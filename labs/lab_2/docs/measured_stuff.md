# A.2
The raw audio stream from the MP34DT06JTR MEMS microphone is captured as 16-bit PCM samples at 16 kHz via the PDM library, as seen in k1-audio-capture.ino. These time-domain samples are accumulated into a 1-second ring buffer (~16,000 samples). Once the buffer is full, it is divided into short overlapping frames (typically 25 ms wide, 10 ms stride) using windowing. Each frame then passes through the MFE/MFCC pipeline: an FFT computes the magnitude spectrum, a bank of Mel-scale triangular filters sums energy across perceptually-spaced frequency bands, and the log of each filterbank energy is taken. For MFE (as used by Edge Impulse), the pipeline stops here — no DCT is applied, unlike full MFCC. The log filterbank energies for each frame are stacked over all 49 frames, producing a 2D tensor of shape 49 × 40 (time frames × Mel bins). This tensor is the "image" the depthwise-separable CNN sees as its input. A frequency-domain representation is superior to raw time-domain input because speech is characterized by spectral shape — the resonant patterns of vowels and consonants — which is invisible in raw amplitude vs. time but clearly separable in the Mel spectrogram. The Mel scale also concentrates resolution where human speech energy is densest (below ~4 kHz), making the features both compact and discriminative. The course textbook (§7.3.3) confirms that a 49×13 MFCC spectrogram is standard for keyword spotting models like DS-CNN; Edge Impulse's MFE block produces a comparable 49×40 tensor using more filterbank bins and skipping the DCT.
